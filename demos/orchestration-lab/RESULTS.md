# Results — findings matrix

Single aggregation point: the walkthrough page copies numbers only from this file. Filled after each captured run from its `run-log.md`; scored against the reference answer keys and rubrics, with bands per `SCORING.md` (every brief is graded on six binary items — four core, one judgment, one completeness — so Pass = all core, Pass+ = core plus judgment, Distinction = all six).

_The public write-up covers the Anthropic arms (fable-orchestrate, opus-orchestrate, and the Claude advisor consult), run identically on every brief: the two orchestrators get the brief verbatim in a fresh headless session, and the advisor arm always runs the same three scripted steps (plain solve, one consult, plain revise). The Codex-led runs (46-orchestrate and advisor-Codex) remain committed here as captured evidence, pending a re-run from a live Codex session before they join the page._

**The matrix.** Five briefs at three rungs of difficulty — moderate (T1 Describe, T2 Estimate, T3 Reviewer reply, all on `projoint::exampleData1`), high (`high-ajr`: replicate and stress the AJR colonial-origins IV), very high (`vhigh-lalonde`: adjudicate Dehejia-Wahba vs Smith-Todd on LaLonde) — each run by all three arms. Fifteen captured runs.

**Scoring columns.** Correctness vs answer key (band + items, per `SCORING.md`); figure-convention compliance (Okabe-Ito, caption-not-title, 300+ dpi, reference levels at zero for T2); tokens; wall-clock; delegations (count, models, efforts); friction events. Claude-Code token "in" = input + cache-creation + cache-read; Codex reports a single tokens-used figure and no USD, so the two platforms' token counts are not directly comparable. Advisor-arm costs are the solve + revise envelopes; the consult step returns text only and is unmetered (stated in each run-log).

## Cross-cutting notes

**Convergence at T1 — a finding, not a shortfall.** All three modes independently arrived at the same design facts (400 respondents, 8 tasks, 2 profiles, 7 attributes, identical level counts) and none asserted "perfect balance." A light Fable lead delegating to Sonnet, a heavy Opus lead delegating to Sonnet, and a medium Codex lead working inline all land on the same numbers. The only gradient is phrasing of the honest imbalance flag. opus and 46 report the exact 1.94pp max deviation and call it negligible. fable reports a 3.08pp spread and calls Driving Time "not indicative of a randomization problem." None reproduced the reference χ²=14.6 test. When the task is computable and single-step, routing shape is a cost/latency choice, not a correctness one.

**Process divergence — how work is routed.** 46-orchestrate spawned zero subagents across all three tiers, working inline even on the judgment tier (its rule is that for compact work, briefing costs more than execution). fable-orchestrate delegated both mechanical tiers wholesale to a low-effort Sonnet (one contract each) and reserved fan-out for T3, splitting estimation to an Opus deep-reasoner and running a blind terra@xhigh Codex peer in parallel. opus-orchestrate kept the reasoning with the lead (0 delegations at T2, 0 Task spawns at T3, only the T1 script sent to Sonnet), which is why it runs longest (24.5 min / 67 turns at T3). The advisor arms are a different shape entirely. Plain solve, one out-of-band consult, one revise. The consult is the whole intervention.

**Product divergence — different answers.** (1) The estimand splits at T2. fable and opus lead with IRR-corrected AMCEs (crime −25.1pp, projoint default). 46 leads with uncorrected AMCEs (crime −16.5pp), labeled. A reader takes away 25pp from two modes and 16pp from the third. (2) 46/T3 marginal means are simply wrong (.541/.458 = 8.3pp gap vs reference .626/.374 = 25.1pp corrected / 16.5 uncorrected). 46 got the binary-invariance argument and the no-overclaim verdict right on a substantively understated number and never disclosed the IRR choice. (3) opus/T3's decorrelated peer was uncalibrated. The lead called `codex exec` without a `--model` pin, so the second opinion ran at the machine's gpt-5.5 default rather than the prescribed gpt-5.6-terra@xhigh. The memo is still the strongest inline product, so the deviation cost process fidelity, not accuracy. The figures split too. Both Codex-authored T3 figures (46, advisor-Codex) are base-R with a custom teal, off the Okabe-Ito convention every Claude-family figure keeps (dpi and caption-not-title still met).

**What each advisor consult caught.** The Claude/Fable consult verified every number against the on-disk script, table, and figure, then added the single decisive argument the solve left implicit (no baseline configuration can raise a rival's displayed AMCE above crime's point estimate, so the softening comes from sampling error, not baselines), surfaced the IRR magnitude (reporting both 16.5/25.1), and fixed a ±notation hazard, which lifted the memo to Distinction. The Codex/Sol consult caught the opposite pair. It flagged an overclaim (crime called "largest span" with 0.251 vs commute's 0.237 untested) and an over-concession (conceding the reviewer's premise when the max−min MM range is reference-invariant), softening the headline to "among the largest, comparable to commuting time."

**Cost gradient.** Within Claude Code the light-lead mode is cheaper than the heavy-lead mode and the gap widens with ambiguity. fable vs opus is $1.88/$2.26 (T1), $2.27/$2.14 (T2), $4.13/$6.73 (T3), $1.00/$1.60 (high), $1.83/$5.01 (very high). opus's own-reasoning routing spends 6.3M input tokens and 24.5 min at T3. The most expensive single cell is the T3 advisor-Claude arm at $7.08 (solve $5.52 + revise $1.57), the two-step protocol that bought the Distinction memo. Codex is off the USD scale (the CLI reports only tokens), but its footprint is a different order. It runs 67k–105k tokens per tier vs fable's 361k–875k and opus's 1.14M–6.28M "input" (Claude Code's figure includes cache reads). Within-platform ranking at the hard tier is fable ($4.13) < opus ($6.73) < advisor-Claude ($7.08). 46 and advisor-Codex sit outside the USD comparison.

**The ladder finding — convergence returns above the judgment tier.** All six ladder runs (three arms × two rungs) score Distinction, and every one costs less than its arm's T3 run. The AJR replication and the LaLonde adjudication are harder *statistics* — IV diagnostics, matching estimators, a genuine methods dispute — but they are canonical problems with computable anchors and literature-settled verdicts, and every arm walks the path. T3 stayed the only separator because its judgment call (declaring a statistical tie between crime and commuting) is original to these data: no published answer exists to converge on. Two practical implications. First, what separates pipelines is not how advanced the methods are but where the un-delegatable judgment sits. Second, compute tracks ambiguity rather than difficulty: the arms spent the most tokens on the brief whose answer was least settled.

**Where the ladder still separates — inference machinery and process.** On the very-high rung the three arms produced the same verdict with different standard-error credibility: opus reached the defensible machinery unaided (HC3 for stratification, refusing five-cluster inference), the advisor arm's solve fell into both SE traps and its consult caught and fixed them, and fable shipped defensible-but-second-best choices (cluster-on-control-id, cluster-by-stratum) that no reviewer challenged because its Codex peer was lost to the headless session boundary. Same band, different assurance depth — the residual gradient a rubric on deliverables does not price. Process also stayed distinctive: fable delegated cheap and fired a peer it never heard back from; opus ran two blind audits in parallel behind a 12-turn lead; the advisor arm's consult independently re-ran every number before critiquing.
