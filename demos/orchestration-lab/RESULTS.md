# Results — findings matrix

Single aggregation point: the walkthrough page copies numbers only from this file. Filled after each captured run from its `run-log.md`; scored against the reference answer keys and rubrics, with bands per `SCORING.md` (every brief is graded on six binary items — four core, one judgment, one completeness — so Pass = all core, Pass+ = core plus judgment, Distinction = all six).

_The page's charts cover the Anthropic arms (fable-orchestrate, opus-orchestrate, and the Claude advisor consult), run identically on every brief: the two orchestrators get the brief verbatim in a fresh headless session, and the advisor arm always runs the same three scripted steps (plain solve, one consult, plain revise). The Codex-led 46-orchestrate arm was re-run on 2026-07-12 at the owner's direction — headless `codex exec`, gpt-5.6-terra · medium, all five briefs, same protocol and rubrics — and its results are integrated below and summarized on the page; it stays out of the USD charts because Codex reports tokens, not dollars. The superseded 2026-07-11 46 captures are in git history. The advisor-Codex arm remains deferred._

**The matrix.** Five briefs at three rungs of difficulty — moderate (T1 Describe, T2 Estimate, T3 Reviewer reply, all on `projoint::exampleData1`), high (`high-ajr`: replicate and stress the AJR colonial-origins IV), very high (`vhigh-lalonde`: adjudicate Dehejia-Wahba vs Smith-Todd on LaLonde) — each run by all three arms. Fifteen captured runs.

**Scoring columns.** Correctness vs answer key (band + items, per `SCORING.md`); figure-convention compliance (Okabe-Ito, caption-not-title, 300+ dpi, reference levels at zero for T2); tokens; wall-clock; delegations (count, models, efforts); friction events. Claude-Code token "in" = input + cache-creation + cache-read; Codex reports a single tokens-used figure and no USD, so the two platforms' token counts are not directly comparable. Advisor-arm costs are the solve + revise envelopes; the consult step returns text only and is unmetered (stated in each run-log).

## Cross-cutting notes

**Convergence at T1 — a finding, not a shortfall.** All modes independently arrive at the same design facts (400 respondents, 8 tasks, 2 profiles, 7 attributes, identical level counts) and none asserts "perfect balance." The gradient is the honest imbalance flag. opus reports the exact 1.94pp max deviation and calls it negligible; fable reports a 3.08pp spread and calls Driving Time "not indicative of a randomization problem"; the advisor arm's revised summary carries the full χ² panel with Driving Time flagged. The 46 re-run reports full frequencies but flags nothing (its superseded capture had reported the exact 1.94pp — thoroughness varies draw to draw). When the task is computable and single-step, routing shape is a cost/latency choice, not a correctness one.

**Process divergence — how work is routed.** In the superseded 2026-07-11 captures 46-orchestrate spawned zero subagents across all three tiers; the 2026-07-12 re-runs route freely — two file owners at T2 (implementer + results analyst), independent checks at T3, a three-role analyst/implementer/verifier split on the IV replication — while still going inline where briefing costs more than execution (T1, and the whole LaLonde adjudication). Same skill, same model, different draws: routing shape is itself run-to-run variance. fable-orchestrate delegated both mechanical tiers wholesale to a low-effort Sonnet (one contract each) and reserved fan-out for T3, splitting estimation to an Opus deep-reasoner and running a blind terra@xhigh Codex peer in parallel. opus-orchestrate kept the reasoning with the lead (0 delegations at T2, 0 Task spawns at T3, only the T1 script sent to Sonnet), which is why it runs longest (24.5 min / 67 turns at T3). The advisor arms are a different shape entirely. Plain solve, one out-of-band consult, one revise. The consult is the whole intervention.

**Product divergence — different answers.** (1) The estimand splits at T2, and it persists across re-runs. fable and opus lead with IRR-corrected AMCEs (crime −25.1pp, projoint default). 46 leads with uncorrected AMCEs (crime −16.5pp), labeled — in the re-run, as the outcome of an explicit integration decision after its two subagents split on exactly this. A reader takes away 25pp from two modes and 16pp from the third. (2) The original 46/T3 capture's marginal means were simply wrong (.541/.458 vs reference .626/.374); the 2026-07-12 re-run reproduces the reference MMs exactly and discloses tau = 0.172, with the fix traceable to the run's own estimand discipline ("one explicitly selected, consistent estimand"). The wrong-MM finding stands as evidence about single-draw risk, not about the current artifact. (3) opus/T3's decorrelated peer was uncalibrated. The lead called `codex exec` without a `--model` pin, so the second opinion ran at the machine's gpt-5.5 default rather than the prescribed gpt-5.6-terra@xhigh. The memo is still the strongest inline product, so the deviation cost process fidelity, not accuracy. The figure conventions mostly converged on re-run: the new 46/T3 figure is base-R with genuine Okabe-Ito hex colors (the old custom teal is gone); advisor-Codex's teal figure stands pending that arm's re-run.

**What each advisor consult caught.** The Claude/Fable consult verified every number against the on-disk script, table, and figure, then added the single decisive argument the solve left implicit (no baseline configuration can raise a rival's displayed AMCE above crime's point estimate, so the softening comes from sampling error, not baselines), surfaced the IRR magnitude (reporting both 16.5/25.1), and fixed a ±notation hazard, which lifted the memo to Distinction. The Codex/Sol consult caught the opposite pair. It flagged an overclaim (crime called "largest span" with 0.251 vs commute's 0.237 untested) and an over-concession (conceding the reviewer's premise when the max−min MM range is reference-invariant), softening the headline to "among the largest, comparable to commuting time."

**Cost gradient.** Within Claude Code the light-lead mode is cheaper than the heavy-lead mode and the gap widens with ambiguity. fable vs opus is $1.88/$2.26 (T1), $2.27/$2.14 (T2), $4.13/$6.73 (T3), $1.00/$1.60 (high), $1.83/$5.01 (very high). opus's own-reasoning routing spends 6.3M input tokens and 24.5 min at T3. The most expensive single cell is the T3 advisor-Claude arm at $7.08 (solve $5.52 + revise $1.57), the two-step protocol that bought the Distinction memo. Codex is off the USD scale (the CLI reports only tokens), but its footprint is a different order. The re-runs span 46k–92k tokens per brief vs fable's 361k–875k and opus's 1.14M–6.28M "input" (Claude Code's figure includes cache reads). Within-platform ranking at the hard tier is fable ($4.13) < opus ($6.73) < advisor-Claude ($7.08). 46 and advisor-Codex sit outside the USD comparison.

**The ladder finding — convergence returns above the judgment tier.** All six Claude-arm ladder runs (three arms × two rungs) score Distinction, and every one costs less than its arm's T3 run. The AJR replication and the LaLonde adjudication are harder *statistics* — IV diagnostics, matching estimators, a genuine methods dispute — but they are canonical problems with computable anchors and literature-settled verdicts, and every arm walks the path. The 46 arm mostly agrees (Distinction on LaLonde; every AJR number exact) with one instructive exception: its AJR memo misses the judgment item — it flags both weak specs but never states that a collapsed first stage neither confirms nor overturns — so it bands Pass on the rung's facts-perfect run. T3 stayed the only brief that separates the Claude arms because its judgment call (declaring a statistical tie between crime and commuting) is original to these data: no published answer exists to converge on. Two practical implications. First, what separates pipelines is not how advanced the methods are but where the un-delegatable judgment sits — and the 46/AJR miss shows the same seam opening on a canonical rung when the memo-writer stops one sentence short of the calibrated ceiling. Second, compute tracks ambiguity rather than difficulty: the arms spent the most on the brief whose answer was least settled.

**Where the ladder still separates — inference machinery and process.** On the very-high rung the three arms produced the same verdict with different standard-error credibility: opus reached the defensible machinery unaided (HC3 for stratification, refusing five-cluster inference), the advisor arm's solve fell into both SE traps and its consult caught and fixed them, and fable shipped defensible-but-second-best choices (cluster-on-control-id, cluster-by-stratum) that no reviewer challenged because its Codex peer was lost to the headless session boundary. Same band, different assurance depth — the residual gradient a rubric on deliverables does not price. Process also stayed distinctive: fable delegated cheap and fired a peer it never heard back from; opus ran two blind audits in parallel behind a 12-turn lead; the advisor arm's consult independently re-ran every number before critiquing.
