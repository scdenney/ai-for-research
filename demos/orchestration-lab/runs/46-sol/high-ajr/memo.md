# Memo: How much weight does the AJR result bear?

The headline result replicates in the 64-country base sample. OLS estimates that a one-unit increase in protection against expropriation risk is associated with 0.522 log points higher 1995 income. Instrumenting institutions with log settler mortality raises the estimate to 0.944. The first stage has the expected negative sign: a one-unit increase in log settler mortality predicts a 0.607-point reduction in the institutions index. Its excluded-instrument F-statistic is 22.95, so the base result is not weakly identified by the conventional F = 10 rule.

The result also survives two common control adjustments, although the instrument becomes less decisive. With latitude included, the OLS and 2SLS estimates are 0.468 and 0.996, respectively, and the first-stage F falls to 13.09. With Africa and Asia indicators, the corresponding estimates are 0.434 and 0.839, with F = 11.01. Thus, across the three specifications that clear the stated strength threshold, the IV coefficient remains positive, substantively large, and appreciably above OLS. The continent-control first stage is only just above the rule-of-thumb cutoff, however, so “not weak” should not be read as “overwhelmingly strong.”

The two sample perturbations do not supply reliable additional causal evidence. After removing Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States, the first-stage coefficient attenuates from -0.607 to -0.391 and F falls to 8.65. The reported 2SLS estimate of 1.281 is therefore weakly identified. Within Africa, the first stage nearly collapses: its coefficient is -0.108 and F is 0.30. The resulting 2SLS estimate of 2.400 is not meaningfully interpretable. Neither weak specification confirms the headline, but neither overturns it: each primarily shows that these data contain too little relevant first-stage variation for that exercise.

The manuscript may claim that it reproduces AJR’s bivariate result in this sample and that the positive, large IV estimate is robust to adding latitude or the two continent indicators under the conventional first-stage diagnostic. It should also disclose that instrument relevance is sensitive to the sample composition and absent within Africa. It may not claim that the exercise establishes a universally robust causal effect, that the Africa-only estimate supports the mechanism, or that removing the neo-Europes leaves the conclusion intact. Even in the stronger specifications, causal interpretation still depends on instrument validity—not merely relevance—especially the exclusion restriction; these estimates do not test that assumption.
