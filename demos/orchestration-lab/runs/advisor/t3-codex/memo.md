# Response to reviewer

Thank you for identifying a useful distinction between an AMCE’s parameterization and the evidence in the conjoint. An AMCE is a contrast against a selected reference level, so changing that reference changes the coefficients displayed, including their signs. It does not, however, change an attribute’s estimated set of level means or its reference-invariant max–min range (equivalently, its largest pairwise AMCE contrast). Re-referencing can create a different apparent importance only if one improperly ranks individual reference-coded coefficients rather than these invariant quantities.

The crime attribute has two levels: communities with crime 20% below or 20% above the national average. With lower crime as the reference, the AMCE for higher crime is -0.251 (95% CI [-0.334, -0.168]); reversing the reference gives +0.251 (95% CI [0.168, 0.334]) for lower crime. These are the same pairwise contrast with its direction reversed. For the multi-level racial-composition attribute, `sensitivity-table.md` reports all four marginal means—50% White/50% Nonwhite, 75% White/25% Nonwhite, 90% White/10% Nonwhite, and 96% White/4% Nonwhite—and their invariant 0.059 max–min range. Thus, alternative coding does not alter the underlying range for either attribute.

We use marginal means as the primary evidence because they require no reference category. The estimates adjust for attenuation from within-respondent response unreliability, estimated from the repeated profile task (tau = 0.172); all reported 95% confidence intervals use respondent-clustered standard errors. The marginal mean is 0.626 (95% CI [0.584, 0.667]) for a profile with lower crime and 0.374 (95% CI [0.333, 0.416]) for one with higher crime, a 0.251 span. Figure `figures/sensitivity.png` shows the corresponding estimates for every randomized level.

We do not treat the 0.251 crime span as a formally established first-place ranking: the commuting-time span is 0.237, and we have not estimated a confidence interval for their difference. The evidence supports a large contrast among the attributes and levels included in this experiment, comparable to commuting time; it does not establish general determinants of community choice beyond the randomized profiles.

We will revise the manuscript claim as follows: *Within the experimentally randomized levels, respondents preferred communities with crime 20% below rather than 20% above the national average. This difference was large and among the largest attribute-level contrasts observed, comparable to commuting time.* We will present marginal means as the primary evidence and AMCEs as reference-dependent contrasts, not standalone rankings of importance.
