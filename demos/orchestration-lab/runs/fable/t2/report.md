# T2 — AMCE results

## Results

Figure 1 reports measurement-error-corrected average marginal component effects (AMCEs) of all seven attributes on the probability that a residential profile is chosen (projoint estimator; N = 400 respondents, 8 paired tasks each). Safety and cost dominate. A community with violent crime 20% above the national average is 25.1 percentage points less likely to be chosen than one 20% below it (95% CI: −33.4 to −16.8). Commuting burden matters almost as much: a 75-minute daily drive reduces choice probability by 23.7 points relative to 10 minutes (−31.0 to −16.5), with monotonic penalties at 45 minutes (−14.1) and 25 minutes (−7.0). Housing costing 40% of pre-tax income lowers support by 19.8 points versus 15% (−27.4 to −12.2). Respondents also reward high-quality schools (9/10 vs. 5/10: +11.6 points) and prefer small towns (+15.8), mixed-use suburbs (+14.6), and rural areas (+13.5) over dense downtowns. Effects of neighborhood partisanship and racial composition are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero. All confidence intervals are based on analytical standard errors clustered at the respondent level, so uncertainty reflects between-respondent variation rather than repeated choices by the same individual.
