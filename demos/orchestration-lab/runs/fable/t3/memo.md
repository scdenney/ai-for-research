# Reply to Reviewer: baseline dependence of the crime finding

We thank the reviewer for pressing on reference-category dependence. We re-estimated all quantities under alternative baselines and added marginal means (MMs), which are baseline-invariant. All estimates below are measurement-error-corrected (projoint; the repeated flipped task); full results are in `sensitivity-table.md` and the figure.

**What we concede.** The reviewer is mechanically correct: AMCEs are defined relative to a reference level, and for multi-level attributes both the coefficients and any importance ranking read off the largest absolute AMCE depend on that choice. Our data show this concretely. Re-baselining the six-level Type of Place attribute to a more central level (City, more residential) shrinks its largest absolute AMCE from 0.158 to 0.115, a 27% drop from relabeling alone, and flips its rank with School Quality (0.116). The revision will therefore not present the default-baseline AMCE ranking as uniquely determined, and will name the reference level wherever an AMCE is reported.

**What is robust.** The Violent Crime Rate attribute is binary (20% less vs. 20% more crime than the national average), so re-baselining can only flip the sign of its AMCE: -0.251 [-0.334, -0.168] becomes +0.251 [0.168, 0.334], with identical magnitude and interval width. There is no baseline under which the crime effect "looks quite different." In the baseline-invariant currency, communities with 20% less crime are chosen with probability 0.626 [0.584, 0.667] versus 0.374 [0.333, 0.416] for 20% more (non-overlapping intervals). Crime's MM spread (0.251) is the largest point estimate of any attribute, but it exceeds daily driving time (0.237) by only 0.014, well within the ~±0.04 precision of the individual MMs; crime and driving time are thus statistically indistinguishable at the top of the ranking, together ahead of housing cost (0.198). The crime effect itself is robust to baseline choice; its precise rank at the very top is not sharply separated from commute.

**What the revision will claim.** "Across the attribute ranges varied in this experiment, violent crime rate was among the strongest predictors of community choice, statistically indistinguishable from driving time at the top of the ranking: profiles with 20% more rather than 20% less crime than the national average were 25 percentage points less likely to be chosen, a difference invariant to reference-category choice." We will report MMs alongside AMCEs throughout, use MM spread for cross-attribute comparisons, and add two caveats: cross-attribute comparisons remain contingent on the particular levels and ranges we operationalized (a ±20% crime contrast is not commensurate with, say, a sixfold range of place types), and attributes with different numbers of levels face different mechanical bounds on their AMCEs. We accordingly avoid any claim that crime is intrinsically the most important determinant of residential choice.
