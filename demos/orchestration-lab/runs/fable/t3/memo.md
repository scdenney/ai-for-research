# Reply to Reviewer: baseline dependence of the crime finding

We thank the reviewer for pressing on reference-category dependence. We re-estimated all quantities under alternative baselines and added marginal means (MMs), which are baseline-invariant. All estimates below are measurement-error-corrected (projoint; the repeated flipped task); full results are in `sensitivity-table.md` and the figure.

**What we concede.** The reviewer is mechanically correct: AMCEs are defined relative to a reference level, and for multi-level attributes both the coefficients and any importance ranking read off the largest absolute AMCE depend on that choice. Our data show this concretely. Re-baselining the six-level Type of Place attribute to the level whose MM sits nearest the sample average shrinks its largest absolute AMCE from 0.158 to 0.115, a 27% drop from relabeling alone, and flips its rank with School Quality (0.116). The revision will therefore not present the default-baseline AMCE ranking as uniquely determined, and will name the reference level wherever an AMCE is reported.

**What is robust.** The Violent Crime Rate attribute is binary (20% less vs. 20% more crime than the national average), so re-baselining can only flip the sign of its AMCE: -0.251 [-0.334, -0.168] becomes +0.251 [0.168, 0.334], with identical magnitude and interval width. There is no baseline under which the crime effect "looks quite different." In the baseline-invariant currency, communities with 20% less crime are chosen with probability 0.626 [0.584, 0.667] versus 0.374 [0.333, 0.416] for 20% more, and crime's MM spread (0.251) is the largest of any attribute, ahead of driving time (0.237) and housing cost (0.198). Crime ranks first in every baseline regime we examined and under both importance metrics. The headline finding is not an artifact of our baseline choices.

**What the revision will claim.** "Across the attribute ranges varied in this experiment, violent crime rate was the strongest predictor of community choice: profiles with 20% more rather than 20% less crime than the national average were 25 percentage points less likely to be chosen, a difference that is invariant to reference-category choice." We will report MMs alongside AMCEs throughout, use MM spread for cross-attribute comparisons, and add two caveats: cross-attribute comparisons remain contingent on the particular levels and ranges we operationalized (a ±20% crime contrast is not commensurate with, say, a sixfold range of place types), and attributes with different numbers of levels face different mechanical bounds on their AMCEs. We will accordingly avoid any claim that crime is intrinsically the most important determinant of residential choice.
