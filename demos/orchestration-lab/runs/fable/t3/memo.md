# Response to the reviewer: reference-category dependence

We thank the reviewer for pressing on this point. The concern is well taken as a general matter, and we have restructured the results so that the headline finding no longer rests on a quantity that depends on our baseline choices.

**What is mechanically true.** The reviewer is correct that an AMCE is defined against a reference level, and that for a multi-level attribute the estimated effect of a given level changes as the reference changes. Our revised Table 2 makes this concrete for Type of Place. Under a "city downtown" baseline, "small town" carries an AMCE of +0.158; re-baselined to "small town," the same comparison is 0 by construction and other levels move accordingly. For attributes with three or more levels, both the magnitudes and the apparent ranking of individual levels are baseline-relative. We no longer present those numbers as if they were baseline-free.

**Why this does not touch the crime finding.** Violent Crime Rate is a binary attribute, with only two levels ("20% less" and "20% more" than the national rate). A binary attribute admits exactly two possible baselines, and switching between them does not change the estimate in any substantive way: it flips the sign and leaves the magnitude and confidence interval identical. The effect is -0.251 [-0.334, -0.168] against a low-crime baseline and +0.251 [0.168, 0.334] against a high-crime baseline (see Table 1). There is no reference choice available to us that could shrink, inflate, or reverse the substantive conclusion. The reviewer's scenario, in which a different baseline makes the effect of crime "look quite different," cannot arise here.

**The baseline-invariant evidence.** We now lead with marginal means, which do not depend on any reference. Profiles with below-average crime are selected 62.6% of the time [58.4, 66.7]; those with above-average crime, 37.4% [33.3, 41.6]. Measured by the spread of marginal means within an attribute, a quantity no baseline choice can alter, crime shows the largest gap of any attribute in the study (0.251), narrowly ahead of commuting time (0.237). Figure 1 shows the marginal means alongside the baseline-dependent AMCEs.

**What the revised manuscript will claim.** We will state that moving a community from below- to above-average violent crime lowers its probability of being chosen by about 25 percentage points, that this is the strongest attribute effect we estimate, and that it is invariant to reference-category choices. We will not claim that crime dominates all other attributes by a wide margin: commuting time is a close second, and we now say so.
