# Reply to the reviewer: reference-category sensitivity of the crime finding

We thank the reviewer for pressing on baselines, and we tested the concern directly. The general mechanics are correct and we concede them. An AMCE is a contrast against an omitted level, so its sign and magnitude are interpretable only relative to that baseline. Our re-analysis shows precisely where this matters and where it does not.

It matters for multi-level attributes. Commute time is a clean case (Table 2). Against a 10-minute baseline the largest coefficient is the 75-minute level (−23.7 pp); against a 75-minute baseline the largest becomes the 10-minute level (+23.7 pp). The individual numbers move with the reference, and so would any "importance" read off a single largest coefficient. This is a presentational artifact, not a change in preferences: every pairwise difference is preserved, so the attribute's full span stays fixed at 23.7 pp whatever the baseline.

It does not matter for the headline attribute. Violent Crime Rate is binary. It admits exactly one contrast, so changing the reference can only flip a sign, never rescale an effect. Under either baseline the corrected difference is 25.1 pp (95% CI [16.8, 33.4]); no baseline exists under which the crime effect shrinks or disappears (Table 1). The reviewer's specific worry, that our crime result is an artifact of baseline choice, cannot hold for a two-level attribute.

The importance ranking is best settled with the baseline-free quantity, marginal means (Table 3; figure, Panel B). Ranking attributes by their within-attribute MM range, a statistic that invokes no reference category, gives: crime 25.1, commute 23.7, housing 19.8, type of place 15.8, school 11.6, and both racial composition and presidential vote under 6 pp. Crime has the widest spread, but only narrowly, and its interval overlaps commute's. The two sit in a statistical tie at the top.

We will revise the claim accordingly. The manuscript will no longer call crime "the" driver of community choice. It will state that a community's violent-crime rate is one of the two strongest drivers, alongside commute time: moving from 20% below to 20% above the national average lowers a community's selection probability by about 25 percentage points (MM 0.63 vs 0.37), the largest baseline-free contrast in the design though statistically indistinguishable from commute time. This claim rests on marginal means and holds under every reference category.
