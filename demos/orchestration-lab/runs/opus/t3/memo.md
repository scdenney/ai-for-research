# Reply to the reviewer: reference-category sensitivity of the crime finding

We thank the reviewer, and tested the concern directly rather than argue it away. The mechanics are correct and we concede them. An AMCE is a contrast against an omitted level, so for a multi-level attribute both the individual coefficients and any importance read off the single largest coefficient move with the baseline. Commute time shows this cleanly (Table 2): against a 10-minute reference the largest coefficient is the 75-minute level (−23.7 pp); against a 75-minute reference it is the 10-minute level (+23.7 pp). We will not rank attributes by baseline-dependent AMCEs.

The reviewer's two worries, however, come apart when applied to crime.

The *magnitude* of the crime effect is not baseline-dependent. Violent Crime Rate is binary, and a two-level attribute admits exactly one contrast, so changing the reference can only flip its sign, never rescale it. The corrected effect is 25.1 pp under either baseline (95% CI [16.8, 33.4]; Table 1), and no reference category exists under which it shrinks or disappears. This is a statement about the ±20% contrast the design actually tests, which is what we claim.

The *ordering* is best settled with the baseline-free quantity, marginal means (Table 3; figure, Panel B). Ranking by within-attribute MM range, which invokes no reference category, puts crime first (25.1 pp), just ahead of commute (23.7) and housing (19.8). A respondent-cluster bootstrap of the range differences shows that crime's lead over the next three attributes is not statistically distinguishable: commute (+1.4 pp, p=0.82), housing (+5.3, p=0.37), and type of place (+9.3, p=0.13). This is failure to detect a difference, not evidence of equality. Crime does separate cleanly from school quality, racial composition, and presidential vote (p≤0.03). If anything this understates crime, because the range metric mechanically favors attributes with more levels, yet binary crime still leads.

We will revise the claim accordingly. The manuscript will no longer call crime "*the*" driver of community choice. It will state that a community's violent-crime rate is among the strongest attributes we measure. Moving from 20% below to 20% above the national average lowers a community's selection probability by about 25 pp (MM 0.63 vs 0.37), the largest single contrast in the design and fully robust to reference-category choice. Its lead over commute time and housing cost, however, lies within sampling error. The claim rests on marginal means and holds under every baseline.
