# Civic Education and Youth Turnout in New Democracies

## Introduction

Whether schools can build durable habits of political participation is an old
question with renewed stakes in democracies that consolidated only recently. We
revisit it with evidence from two such cases, Veremia and Talenia, and argue
that classroom civic education leaves a measurable but short-lived imprint on
youth turnout.

The starting point is straightforward. Civic education produces a modest,
short-lived increase in youth turnout, on the order of three to four points,
that fades within two election cycles [@lindqvist2019]. We take this as the
baseline effect against which other interventions should be judged.

## The participation gap

A recurring worry is that digital media widen rather than narrow the
participation gap between richer and poorer young voters [@othen2022]. If that
is right, then civic-education effects measured a decade ago may overstate what
schools can achieve today.

Compulsory voting is sometimes offered as a blunt alternative. Beyond lifting
turnout mechanically, compulsory voting also raises citizens' political
knowledge [@ferreira-nair2021], which suggests that the act of voting itself can
be educative.

A second line of work emphasizes the social roots of participation. Higher
social trust causes higher turnout across democracies [@osei2020], so civic
programs that build trust among classmates may pay off at the ballot box years
later.

## Design

We estimate the effect of a civic-education reform using a conjoint design,
following standard practice for decomposing multidimensional choices
[@hainmueller2014]. Earlier descriptive work in Veremia anticipated several of
these patterns [@vance2018].

## Findings

The reform raised self-reported turnout intention among first-time voters, with
the largest movement among students from civically disengaged households. The
effect attenuated sharply by the second post-reform election, consistent with
the baseline in the literature.

## Conclusion

Civic education is worth doing, but it is not a substitute for the institutional
arrangements that keep young citizens voting once the novelty wears off.
